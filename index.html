Lesson Walkthrough

This page turns the simulator into the actual lesson. The main idea is that the car’s average speed between two cameras is an average rate of change, while the speedometer shows an instantaneous rate of change.

The Mean Value Theorem connects those two ideas: if the car’s motion is smooth, then the car’s instantaneous speed must equal its average speed at least once during the measured interval.

What does AROC mean?

Average Rate of Change measures how much something changes over an entire interval. In this project, the quantity is the car’s position, and the interval is from Camera A to Camera B.

AROC = change in position / change in time = distance between cameras / time between cameras
2

What does IROC mean?

Instantaneous Rate of Change measures the rate at one exact moment. In this project, the instantaneous rate of change is the car’s exact speed at a specific time.

IROC = s'(t) = the speedometer reading at one exact moment
3

What does the Mean Value Theorem say?

If the car’s position function s(t) is continuous and differentiable from Camera A to Camera B, then there is at least one time c where the instantaneous speed equals the average speed.

s'(c) = [s(b) − s(a)] / [b − a]

On the simulator’s position-time graph, this is shown by orange tangent lines at the MVT moments having about the same slope as the secant line from Camera A to Camera B.

4

How does the simulator show it?

The simulator lets you pass both cameras slowly, speed up in the middle, and then slow down again. The cameras may not catch the car speeding at the exact camera points, but the average speed between the cameras can still prove speeding.

Camera Evidence

The car can be slow at Camera A and Camera B, so the cameras alone might not visually catch the speeding moment.

Calculus Evidence

If the average speed is above the speed limit, MVT proves the car’s instantaneous speed was above the limit somewhere between the cameras.
